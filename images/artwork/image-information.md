Title: Planting Rice



Artist: Fernando Amorsolo



Year: 1921



Medium: Oil on canvas



Location (if applicable): National Museum of Fine Arts, Manila



Background:

This painting depicts Filipino farmers working together in a rice field under bright sunlight, capturing a peaceful and harmonious rural scene. Women are shown planting rice seedlings while men prepare the land, all set against a backdrop of lush mountains and a glowing sky.



Fernando Amorsolo, known for his mastery of light, used warm tones and soft illumination to highlight the beauty of everyday Filipino life. Created during the American colonial period, the artwork reflects an idealized vision of rural life, emphasizing hard work, community, and a deep connection to nature. It also reinforces national pride by presenting the Filipino farmer as dignified and integral to the country’s identity.

Title: The Blood Compact 



Artist: Juan Luna



Year: 1886



Medium: Oil on canvas



Location (if applicable): Malacañang Palace, Manila



Background:

This painting shows the sandugo or blood compact ceremony that happened in 1565 between Miguel López de Legazpi and Datu Sikatuna. In this ritual, both men mixed their blood in a drink as a sign of friendship and agreement.



Juan Luna created this artwork during the Spanish colonial period to highlight the dignity and capability of Filipinos. He portrayed Datu Sikatuna as composed and equal to Legazpi, going against the negative stereotypes of natives at the time. The painting emphasizes early cooperation between Filipinos and Spaniards and reflects the growing sense of national identity.

image: spolarium.jpg

Title: Spoliarium



Artist: Juan Luna



Year: 1884



Medium: Oil on canvas



Location (if applicable): National Museum of Fine Arts, Manila



Background:

This painting portrays a dramatic scene in a Roman spoliarium, where fallen gladiators are dragged and stripped of their weapons and armor after combat. The dark, chaotic setting emphasizes suffering, death, and the brutality of the spectacle.



Juan Luna created this masterpiece during the Spanish colonial period while studying in Europe. Beyond its historical subject, the painting serves as a powerful allegory of the oppression and suffering of Filipinos under colonial rule. It reflects Luna’s strong sense of nationalism and desire for justice, symbolizing the struggle against injustice and the fight for freedom.



The artwork gained international recognition after winning the first gold medal at the 1884 Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes in Madrid, proving the artistic excellence of Filipinos and contributing to the rise of national consciousness.

Title: Fruit Gatherer



Artist: Fernando Amorsolo



Year: 1950



Medium: Oil on board



Location (if applicable): Vargas Museum, University of the Philippines



Background:

This painting depicts a Filipina woman in a rural setting, holding a basket filled with freshly gathered fruits while surrounded by nature. The scene highlights the abundance of the land and the simple, everyday life in the Philippine countryside.



Fernando Amorsolo, known for his mastery of light and rural themes, used warm colors and natural sunlight to create a peaceful and idealized image. The woman is portrayed as the “dalagang Filipina,” representing traditional Filipino beauty and modesty.



Created during the mid-20th century, the artwork reflects Amorsolo’s vision of Filipino identity—emphasizing harmony with nature, agricultural life, and national pride through the dignity of ordinary people.

Title: Hope in the Ruins of Manila



Artist: Fernando Amorsolo



Year: 1945



Medium: Oil on canvas



Location (if applicable): Private collection



Background: 

This painting depicts the aftermath of the Battle of Manila during World War II, showing a devastated city filled with ruins, fallen bodies, and destruction. At the center, a mother carrying her child stands out, symbolizing protection, resilience, and hope amid chaos.



Fernando Amorsolo created this work after witnessing the horrors of war, including the destruction of Manila and the suffering of Filipino civilians. His wartime paintings marked a shift from his usual peaceful rural scenes to more dramatic and emotional subjects that documented the realities of conflict.



The strong contrast between light and darkness highlights the theme of hope surviving in the midst of despair. The mother and child represent the enduring Filipino spirit and the possibility of renewal despite tragedy, reflecting national resilience after one of the most devastating periods in Philippine history.
